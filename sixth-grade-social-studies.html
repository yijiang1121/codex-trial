6th Grade Social Studies: History–Social Science Framework

Engage students in global stories that highlight geography, culture, and the rise of ancient civilizations while practicing the inquiry skills called for in the History–Social Science Framework.

What We Explore in 6th Grade

Sixth graders travel across time and continents to discover how people adapted to their environments, formed belief systems, built governments, and exchanged ideas. Each unit blends geography, economics, civics, and history to show how societies grow more complex.

  • Trace how rivers, landforms, and resources shape where people settle and how they live.
  • Compare belief systems, civic structures, and cultural traditions across ancient regions.
  • Analyze primary sources to understand multiple perspectives on the same historical moment.
  • Build academic vocabulary that helps students explain cause and effect, continuity, and change.

Unit Highlights

Unit 1: Mapping Our World

Students create layered maps that show physical geography, climate zones, and trade routes. They practice latitude and longitude skills by planning an imaginary expedition around the globe.

Unit 2: Early Humans & Agricultural Revolutions

Learners investigate how the shift from foraging to farming transformed daily life. Stations focus on technology innovations, food surplus, and the first permanent communities.

Unit 3: Ancient River Civilizations

Case studies of Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus River Valley, and China help students identify common traits of civilizations—organized governments, belief systems, social classes, and writing.

Unit 4: Mediterranean Connections

Students explore the exchange of ideas across the Mediterranean Basin, from Greek philosophy and democratic ideals to Roman engineering and law.

Inquiry Skills in Action

  • Source sleuthing: examine who created a document, why it was produced, and what evidence it provides.
  • Timeline design: organize events visually to see how innovations spread and intersect.
  • Academic conversation: use accountable talk stems to build on classmates’ ideas and debate respectfully.
  • Claim and evidence writing: support conclusions with facts from texts, visuals, and data sets.

Classroom Connections

Connect social studies content to literacy, science, and art. Pair informational texts with hands-on labs, such as designing water wheels to model ancient irrigation systems, or compose travel journal entries from the viewpoint of a young trader visiting different regions.
